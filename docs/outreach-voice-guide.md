# Adapt Outreach Voice & Structure Guide

This guide governs how first-contact outreach is written. It is distilled from
Gabe's and Adam's feedback ("Outreach Notes") and from real cold emails Adam has
sent (see `docs/Outreach-emails.md`). Use it for every outreach draft.

## The job of a first email

Get **one** of: a reply, a call, a completed diagnostic, or permission to share
something useful. **Do not try to sell the engagement in the first email.**

## Lead positioning hypothesis — business worth / value

The strongest hook is **enterprise value and owner freedom**, not process
language. Owners may nod along to "alignment, cadence, handover, knowledge
capture" and still not act because they're busy. The value angle is what stops
them:

- The business may be worth less than it should be.
- Owner dependency creates a **discount** on the business.
- A business that can run without everything routing through the owner is worth
  more — and opens up freedom, scale, exit and transition options.

Lead with value/worth. Process and capability are the *how*, mentioned lightly.

## Structure (short, plain-spoken, low-friction)

1. **Proof we researched them** — one specific line about how the business grew
   (specialist relationships, senior people, founder/partner knowledge, multi-
   location, etc.). This earns the next sentence.
2. **The pattern + the value cost** — in businesses like this, a lot of value
   sits in a few heads; that makes the firm more dependent on key people than the
   owner wants, which affects freedom, scale options and eventual value.
3. **One line on what Adapt does** — helps privately owned Australian firms
   reduce that dependency and make the business more valuable *without losing
   what made it work*.
4. **Low-friction first step — the free diagnostic.** Not Lumia Design. The
   diagnostic gives them a clear read on their biggest dependency/value gaps and
   is an easy way to judge whether Adapt is credible.
5. **A soft ask** — e.g. "Would it be useful if I sent that through?" / "Is that
   worth exploring?" / offer a coffee in Perth.

## Voice rules

- Short. Plain-spoken. Specific to the company.
- Confident, speaking from authority — not pitchy, not defensive.
- Research-led but not verbose. No long justification paragraphs.
- Value-first, not product-first.
- Avoid jargon unless it's normal owner language.
- Address the named decision-maker by first name.
- Roughly 90–150 words. One clear ask.

## Do not

- **Do not lead with Lumia Design** or any paid engagement as the first step.
- **Do not use defensive AI-style contrast lines**, e.g. "not generic coaching
  and not just software", "we are not a technology implementation firm". They
  read like justification. Speak from authority instead.
- Don't stack benefits or dump the full methodology.
- Don't open with a generic pitch; open with proof you researched them.

## Reference — Adapt's own line (use sparingly, reworded)

> At Adapt we help you build a valuable business with clarity and accountability,
> improved leadership, a healthy culture, systemised processes and a defined path
> to exit or scale — without the business being dependent on you.

Keep that as the underlying promise, but say it in one plain sentence, tilted
toward **value/worth and owner freedom**.

## The three variants we generate

Each company gets three short drafts, all following this guide, differing only in
angle/tone:

1. **Value & worth** — leads hardest on enterprise value and the owner-dependency
   discount.
2. **Plain-spoken (Adam's voice)** — local, warm, "happy to grab a coffee in
   Perth" register; research-led and human.
3. **Permission-first** — shortest; proves research, names the value gap, and
   asks permission to send the free diagnostic.

All three: value-first, free diagnostic as the first step, one low-friction ask,
no pitch dump, no defensive lines.
